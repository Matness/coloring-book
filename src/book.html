Coloring Book of Group Theory

Mathematics is about more than just numbers. Numbers can be beautiful, but they are only one way to talk about their underlying mathematical concepts. In this "book" our language of math will be visual and focus on shapes and patterns.

Group theory is the study of algebraic structures, and with it we can explore symmetry.

This is a coloring book.

Shapes & Symmetries

We have intuitive ideas of symmetry. Some shapes look "more symmetric" than others. For example, a square is "more symmetric" than a rectangle.

But what does that mean? We can be more precise. We can even count how much symmetry a shape has.

Rotations // Cyclic Groups

An equilateral triangle can be rotated ⅓ of the way around a circle and appear unchanged.

If the triangle were instead rotated by an arbitrary amount, like ¼ of the way around a circle, it would then appear changed, since it is oriented differently.

We can still rotate that triangle by more than ⅓ of the way around a circle without changing it. It can be rotated by twice that much - ⅔ of the way around the circle - or by 3 times that much, which is all the way around the circle.

We can keep rotating - by 4 times that much, 5 times that much, 6 times that much... and keep going towards infinity. The triangle seems to have an infinite number of rotations, but after rotating 3 times and reaching a full turn the rotations become repetitive.

There are only 3 unique rotations for a triangle. We'll talk about them by referring to the rotations that are less than a full turn.

Other shapes, not just equilateral triangles, have the same 3 rotations. For this reason, we can say they're all in the same cyclic "group" (C3).

Now that we can count rotations, we can be more precise when we say a square has more symmetry than a rectangle.

We can also see that a square has more rotational symmetry than a triangle, which in turn has more symmetry than a rectangle: A square has 4 unique rotations, while a triangle has 3, and a rectangle has only 2.

We don't need to stop at 4 rotations. We can find shapes with 5 rotations, 6 rotations, 7, 8, ... and keep going. When we get to infinity, we have a circle.

...

We need a better way to talk about this. We say a shape has rotational symmetry of order n if it has n unique rotations. This means the shape can be rotated 1/n of the way around the circle without changing its appearance.

...

For example, a triangle can be rotated by ⅓ of the way around the circle so a triangle has order 3. A square can be rotated ¼ of the way around the circle without changing, so it has order 4.

We can say the same for shapes that are not regular polygons.

When shapes have the same rotational order, they share a cyclic group. We can use the name C3 to talk about the group of shapes that have 3 rotations. We can also use the name C4 for the shapes that have 4 rotations, C5 for the shapes that have 5 rotations, and so on.
